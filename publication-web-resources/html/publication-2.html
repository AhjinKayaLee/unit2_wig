In 17th century France,

wigs were seen as a symbol of authority and status. Louis XIV (the Sun King) made wigs even more colourful and massive as a fashion item, which became an important indicator of status and power in the French aristoc-racy of the time. Although Louis XIV was called the ‘King of Fashion’, he was actually short in stature and had many physical flaws. To compound the juxtaposition, from the age of 17, his hair receded rapidly, leaving him almost bald. Louis XIV’s favourite thing to wear was a large, long, curly wig. The voluminous wig, which covered his shoulders, not only hid his baldness but also soared over his head, making him appear much taller. Following in the footsteps of the king, the nobility began to wear wigs, and they became widely popular. Some say that at first, the king looked awk-ward in a wig due to the lack of skill in wig-making, so the nobles joined in to save his face. As Louis XIV consoli-dated royal power and established a centralised

state sys-tem, he used the wig to symbolical-ly convey authority and discipline to his nobles.The wig was considered a must-have attire among people of high social status and spread throughout Europe. In the late 18th century, the style became simplified with the French Revolution, signifying revolutionary ideas and rebellion against aristocratic society.
